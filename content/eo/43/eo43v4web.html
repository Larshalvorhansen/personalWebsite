A PCA Analysis of Norway's 500 Largest Companies in 2025

Week 43

This report presents a principal component analysis (PCA) examining the geographic, sectoral, and financial characteristics of Norway's 500 largest companies. In brief, the analysis reveals that nearly all the variance (85.8%) is explained by just the first three principal components (PC1, 2 and 3), indicating that large Norwegian firms are highly similar and follow predictable patterns. The dominant drivers explaining their differences are economic sector (primary, secondary, or tertiary), profitability, and thirdly, the company age.


The dataset comprises 495 Norwegian companies (after data cleaning) and 17 features: financial metrics (revenue, assets, equity, profitability ratio, ROA, ROE, profit margin, asset turnover), company characteristics (age, employee count, profitability status), geographic data (distance from Oslo), and sector classification (primary, secondary, tertiary). Missing data (34%) was imputed using mean values, and all features were normalized to a [0,1] range using min-max scaling before PCA was performed.


Scree and cumulative variance plot showing PC1-3 explain 85.8% of variance.


As visible in the scree plot, these three principal components explain almost all of the variance: PC1 captures 45.3%, PC2 adds 28.8%, and PC3 contributes 11.7%. Since the first three components capture so much of the variance, large Norwegian companies cluster into distinct, behaviorally similar groups. The dimensionality reduction from 17 to 3 dimensions is highly efficient, allowing for clear visualization in three-dimensional space. Feel free to view it here in this interactive plot!


PCA Loadings Heatmap showing feature contributions to each principal component.


PC1 (45.3%): Economic Sector and Profitability Divide: From the PCA loadings chart, the three strongest contributors to PC1 are profitability (+0.63), secondary sector score (+0.46), and tertiary sector score (-0.61). Thus, PC1 captures the major divide between profitable secondary-sector firms (industry, construction, manufacturing) on the positive side and tertiary-sector firms (services, finance, trade) on the negative side. The single strongest predictor of a company's other traits is whether it operates in goods production (secondary) or services (tertiary).


PC2 (28.8%): Company Maturity and Establishment: Company age impacts PC2 strongly at +0.61. The three sector indicators from PC1 also remain influential, confirming their importance. Older, established tertiary firms (such as DNB and Telenor) stand apart from newer secondary-sector firms.


PC3 (11.7%): Primary Sector and Proximity to Oslo: After PC1 and PC2 extract their variance, the remaining dominant signal distinguishes primary-sector firms and headquarters distance from Oslo. This aligns with intuition: large Norwegian companies typically headquarter in the capital, while primary-sector firms (resource extraction) are located far from Oslo, often offshore (oil platforms), along the coast (wild fishing, aquaculture), or near hydropower sites (e.g., Norsk Hydro).


PCA Biplot (PC1 vs PC2). Arrow length reflects feature importance; point clusters indicate company similarity.


The biplot is useful for visualizing correlations between companies (points) and features (arrows). Small angles indicate high positive correlation; approximately 90° means no correlation. In our biplot, profitability correlates strongly with both sector type and company age. Most large Norwegian firms cluster tightly, suggesting high similarity across dimensions. However, imputing 34% missing data with averages have most likely artificially tightened the clusters. Future analyses would benefit from more complete data collection.


Key Insights: The primary sector is small but dominated by Equinor (~70% of total primary-sector by market cap). The secondary sector shows wide performance dispersion: successes like Kongsberg Gruppen coexist with struggling players like REC Silicon. The tertiary (services) sector dominates in company count, firms like DNB and Telenor being in the lead. A firms age intrestingly correlates positively with profits, especially in Norway's services sector. Established players like Storebrand enjoy structural advantages over newcomers.


Primary-sector firms are located farthest from Oslo, tied to resource geography. Secondary and tertiary firms cluster around Oslo (and secondarily Bergen, Trondheim, Stavanger), though only Oslo distance was modeled here. Size metrics cluster tightly, suggesting that Norwegian firms maybe follow similar asset allocation patterns, possibly driven by strict taxation incentivizing efficient capital use. But that is only speculation and would be interesting to reserearch further in the future.


In conclusion: Norway's corporate landscape is remarkably homogeneous along these three core axes: sector, profitability, and age, with geography playing a fourth and intuitive role. The extreme concentration of variance in just three components could underscore the structural uniformity among Norway's largest firms, that could be shaped by shared resource dependence (especially on oil), a mature service industry, strict government regulation and a natural clustering in financial metrics related to company size.


Interesting topics for further analysis:

  • Causes of the link between company age and profitability in Norway's tertiary sectors
  • Impact of taxation on asset allocation homogeneity
  • Inclusion of regional city distances (Bergen, Stavanger, etc.) in future geographic models
  • Robustness checks using alternative imputation methods (e.g., KNN, iterative imputation)
  • Sector-specific PCA to uncover sub-patterns within secondary heterogeneity
