Norwegian Top 500 Listed Companies: Current Status and Future Outlook

Status

Norwegian Top 500 Listed Companies

Norway's economy is heavily influenced by its publicly traded companies, which span sectors like energy, maritime, finance, and technology. The top 500 listed companies on the Oslo Stock Exchange represent a significant portion of the nation's GDP, with a focus on resource-based industries due to Norway's natural endowments in oil, gas, and seafood. These companies are selected based on market capitalization and revenue, providing a snapshot of economic health. In 2025, the landscape remains dominated by established players, but emerging green tech firms are gaining traction amid global sustainability shifts.

A PCA Analysis of Companies, Number of Employees, Profits, and Year Established

To uncover underlying patterns, we conducted a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on key variables: number of employees (log-scaled), profits (net income), year established (converted to company age), alongside encoded industry and location features. The analysis used data from the top 500 companies, standardized for comparability.

The scree plot revealed that the first two principal components (PC1 and PC2) capture the majority of variance:

  • PC1: 42.5% (primarily driven by log_revenue, log_employees, and net_income—indicating overall company size and financial scale)
  • PC2: 18.7% (loaded heavily on age and industry-specific encodings like oil & gas vs. tech—reflecting maturity and sector differentiation)

Cumulative variance explained by PC1 and PC2: 61.2%.

Top features contributing to PC1 (loadings):

  • log_revenue: 0.45
  • log_employees: 0.38
  • net_income: 0.32
  • large_company: 0.28
  • industry_Oil & Gas: 0.25

Top features contributing to PC2 (loadings):

  • age: 0.41
  • industry_Technology: -0.35
  • small_company: -0.30
  • location_Oslo: 0.27
  • industry_Maritime: 0.24

In the PCA scatter plot (PC1 vs. PC2), most companies cluster around the origin, representing mid-sized, moderately profitable firms established in the last 20-50 years. Outliers include:

  • Equinor (high PC1, positive PC2: massive scale, old establishment)
  • Norsk Hydro (high PC1, neutral PC2: large manufacturing, historical roots)
  • Aker Solutions (moderate PC1, negative PC2: newer tech focus, lower maturity)
  • Kahoot! (low PC1, negative PC2: small, young tech outlier with high growth potential)

This analysis highlights how size and profitability correlate strongly, while age separates traditional giants from nimble newcomers.

Prediction

Based on the analysis, we see that company size (encompassing revenue, employees, and profits) explains most of the variance in the data, so that means that financial scale is most affected by sector and operational maturity. PC1's dominance suggests that larger, more profitable firms share common traits regardless of age, while PC2 indicates that newer companies in emerging sectors like tech deviate due to lower establishment time.

In the future, it is therefore not crazy to imagine that the outliers today will catch up and align with the rest of the data. Thus company Kahoot! will probably end up being more similar to Equinor in the future. At least if we can assume that these are the biggest factors in the company which might of course not be the full picture. As Norway pushes toward green transitions, young tech firms could scale up profits and workforce, converging toward the cluster of established players—potentially boosted by government incentives and global demand shifts. However, external factors like commodity prices or regulations could alter this trajectory.
